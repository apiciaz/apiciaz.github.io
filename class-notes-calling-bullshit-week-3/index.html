Class Notes: Calling Bullshit Week 3

2020-11-13

Week 3: The natural ecology of bullshit

Where does bullshit spread? Why are people receptive to it?

Lecture key takeaways

  • Correlation is not causation.
  • Tips for avoiding mistaking correlation for causation:
    • ommitted common cause
      • E.X. The number of storks correlates with the number of babies, but both are correlated to the size of countries.
    • multiple contributing causes
      • E.X. Trump has 8x as many streaming viewers because of 1) his popularity, 2) better streaming technology.
    • what happens later does not have to be the result of what happens first.
      • E.X. Sale of infant seats is correlated to the number of newborn babies. Do baby seats cause baby births?

Readings key takeaways

Gordon Pennycook et al. (2015) On the reception and detection of pseudo-profound bullshit. Judgement and Decision Making 10:549-563

The main focus of this investigation is whether people can detect bullshit, who is more susceptible to it, and why. Authors are particularly interested in pseudo-profound bullshit, composed of vague words yet without actual meaning, not meant to inform but to impress . E.X. "Hidden meaning transforms unparalleled abstract beauty."

The authors then propose two potential mechanisms that explain people's general receptivity to bullshit:

  • a default sense of meaningfulness (uncritically open mind)
  • confuse vagueness for profundity (inability to summon skepticism)
    They want to find out if there are cognitive factors consistently associated with individual ability to distinguish bullshit from non-bullshit. They then design experiments around cognitive style (intuitive vs. analytical), supernatural beliefs, religiosity, conspiratorial ideation, copmlementary and alternative medicine.

To measure the ability to spot bullshit, the researcherss build a profundity rating on a scale from 1 (not at all profound) to 5 (very profound) by asking participants to rate statemetns.

  • Study 1 establishes a positive relation between high ratings and receptivity to bullshit.
  • Study 2 looks into the negative correlation between analytical thinking (measured by scores on heuristic and biases tests) and receptivity to bullshit (BSR).
  • Study 3 investigates the link among analytical thinking, ability to detect conflicts, and ability to detect bullshit.
  • Study 4 is a finer version of Study 3. It confirms the negative correlation between BSR and scores on heuristic and biases tests, but shows that analytical thinking is not significantly related to bullshit sensitivity (measured by the difference between BSR of genuinely profound quotes and that of pseudo-profound statements).

The above studies demonstrate that receptivity to bullshit can be measured, but more work is needed to refine BSR by clarifying the difference between pseudo-profound statements and genuinely meaningful ones. Additional studies to isolate the effects of cognitive variables would also be interesting.
Further researches could explore other types of bullshit, or focus on aspects of the statements (source, wording, syntax, context, etc) and investigate which part of the statement cues a reader to stop and think.

Personally, my biggest takeaway is the danger of an uncritically open mind. We gather information to analyze, test hypotheses, and form opinions. In an age where we are inundated with excessive information, it is essential to stay vigilant and absorb information selectively and reflectively.
