Week 2: Spotting bullshit

How to spot bullshit in the open world?

Lecture key takeaways

"The price for truth is eternal vigilance."
When you hear something, always think about:

  • Effect sizes: watch out for statements that sound too good to be true
  • Fermi estimation: estimate using orders of magnitude
    • E.X. What is the total expenditure of the U.S. food stamp program?
      ? % of the population get food stamps: 1% 10% 100%
      $? /person/year: $100 $1000 $10000
      What's the total U.S. population?
  • Confirmation bias: less likely to question when the evidence is in line with existing beliefs
  • Check plausibility: watch out for unfiar comparison; is there more sugar in apple juice or freshly squeezed orange juice?
  • Form multiple hypotheses

The most difficult part is to always be vigilant in daily life. Whenever encounter a why or how question, try to think hard and come up with multiple hypotheses.

Readings key takeaways

Carl Sagan 1996 The Fine Art of Baloney Detection. Chapter 12 in Sagan (1996) The Demon-Haunted World

The train of thought starts with Sagan's late parents and his unwillingness to take comfort in false promises even though he misses them dearly. Then comes Hume and Paine's take on infidelity- "professing to believe one does not believe." To Huxley, dropping the pretense of believing things without evidence is fundamental to morality. Sagan sees a broader implication of those philosophers' statements, especially in a highly commercialized world.

He provides a kit of principles that help us to "construct, and to understand, a reasoned argument and to recognize a fallacious or fraudulent argument." In short, it's a toolkit for skeptical thinking.

  • independent confirmation of the facts
  • substantive debate by knowledgeable proponents from all perspectives
  • form multiple hypotheses and think of tests to disprove each alternative
  • stay detached from your hypotheses
  • quantify whenever there is a measurement
  • choose the more straightforward explanation when two ideas explain the data equally well
  • always ask how a hypothesis can be falsified
    Not-to-do's
  • absence of evidence is not evidence of absence
  • inconsistency (different standards and treatments on the same matter in two situations)
  • non sequitor - it doesn't follow.
  • post hoc, ergo propter hoc - it happened after, so it was the consequence.
  • small numbers
  • simplify an argument, so it's easier to attack
  • extreme dichotomy

What I like the most about Sagan's article is that he acknowledges that applied out of context, this tool kit can be abused as a rote replacement or lazy getaway for actual thinking.
